Common questions

Real objections, direct answers

A CTO owns the technology function in its entirety: engineering teams, architecture, delivery, vendor management, board liaison. That is a full-time role at scaling companies. Adding AI strategy, governance, and transformation architecture ownership on top of it without additional support is how you end up with a CTO who is accountable for AI systems they cannot realistically supervise. A Fractional CAIO takes that specific layer off the CTO's plate and owns it with genuine authority. The two roles are complementary, not competing.
A traditional management consultant produces a report and leaves. You receive findings, recommendations, and a slide deck. Your team then has to interpret that report, translate it into a plan, and execute it without the person who produced the analysis in the room. A Rvelate Sprint produces an architecture your team owns, an action plan your team built alongside Chidi, and a named accountable owner who is responsible for what the Day 2 session delivers. The output is designed for execution, not for reading.
The AI Transformation Architecture Assessment, completed before Day 1, identifies exactly which of the six architecture dimensions caused your previous initiative to stall. The Sprint does not add another initiative on top of the ones that have already stalled — it diagnoses the architectural reasons why they stalled and produces the integration architecture that was missing when they were launched. Most failed AI initiatives fail for the same three or four architectural reasons. The Sprint names them, maps them, and gives your team a specific plan for addressing them.
If you have any AI tools operating inside your organisation — even at a single team level — you are already in an AI adoption posture. The question is not whether you are ready but whether you have the governance and architecture to make adoption coherent and governable. If you genuinely have no AI adoption and no near-term pressure to address it, the Sprint is probably not the right starting point. The 20-minute fit call will tell you quickly.
The Sprint produces an Integration Architecture Document, not a migration plan. The architecture is designed to work with your existing enterprise framework — SAFe, Scrum, LeSS, or a combination — rather than replacing it. The 90-Day Action Plan prioritises the highest-impact changes within your current operating context. Massive infrastructure change is not the output. Architectural clarity about where the gaps are and a prioritised plan for closing them is the output.
A named internal champion — typically the CTO or a senior architect — commits a maximum of two to three hours per week during the Sprint. This includes: completing the Assessment before Day 1, participating in Day 1 (four to six hours), reviewing draft architecture documents during the production gap, and attending Day 2 (three to four hours). The schedule is structured and agreed in advance. The process is designed to protect your team's time, not consume it.
You own the deliverables and the choice of what to do next. Some clients execute the 90-Day Action Plan with their existing team. Others engage Rvelate on a Fractional CAIO retainer for ongoing AI strategy ownership and governance oversight, starting from £25,000 per month. Others take the architecture to an incoming or existing CTO and hand it over. The Sprint is designed so that all three paths are viable — your team is not dependent on Rvelate to use what it produces.
The AI Transformation Architecture Sprint is £10,000. Fixed fee. No day rates, no scope variations, no additional charges. The Fractional CAIO Retainer, for clients who choose to continue after the Sprint, starts from £25,000 per month — pricing by conversation depending on scope and commitment. Both are fixed-fee engagements anchored to outcomes.
